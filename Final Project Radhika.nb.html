Project Introduction:

This research endeavors to delve into the dynamics influencing life expectancy across 193 countries during the 2000-2015 period. While prior studies have explored demographic and economic aspects, this project expands the scope by incorporating crucial factors like immunization, human development index, and other health indicators. Motivated by the gaps in considering immunization and human development index in previous research, and limitations of single-year data, this study aims for a comprehensive analysis.

The dataset, sourced from the Global Health Observatory (GHO) under the World Health Organization (WHO) and United Nations, encompasses life expectancy and various health factors, with economic data obtained from the United Nations. Focused on 2000-2015, the project consolidates data into a single dataset with 22 columns and 2938 rows, representing 20 predicting variables categorized into immunization, mortality, economic, and social factors.

Addressing the importance of data accuracy, missing values were meticulously handled using the R software. The final dataset excluded countries with substantial missing data, ensuring robustness for analysis. The collaborative efforts of Deeksha Russell and Duan Wang facilitated data collection from WHO and United Nations websites.

Key Questions Explored in the Project:

  1. Healthcare Expenditure and Life Expectancy:
    • Should countries with life expectancy below 65 increase healthcare expenditure for enhanced lifespan?
  2. Mortality Rates and Life Expectancy:
    • How do infant and adult mortality rates shape life expectancy?
  3. Correlation with Lifestyle Factors:
    • Is there a positive or negative correlation between life expectancy and eating habits, lifestyle, exercise, smoking, and alcohol consumption?
  4. Impact of Schooling on Lifespan:
    • What influence does schooling have on individuals’ lifespan?
  5. Relationship with Alcohol Consumption:
    • Does life expectancy exhibit a positive or negative relationship with alcohol consumption?
  6. Population Density and Life Expectancy:
    • Do densely populated countries tend to experience lower life expectancy?
  7. Immunization Coverage Impact:
    • What impact does immunization coverage have on life expectancy?
  8. Impact of Predicting Factors on Life Expectancy:
    • How significantly do selected predicting factors influence life expectancy?
    • Which variables emerge as pivotal contributors to life expectancy variations?

This project seeks to offer insightful findings into the intricate factors affecting global life expectancy, providing guidance for policymakers and healthcare professionals in prioritizing areas for improvement to enhance the well-being and longevity of populations.

Now, let’s state the null and alternative hypotheses for the coefficient of “Total Expenditure” and interpret the results:

Hypotheses: - Null Hypothesis (\(H_0\)): The percentage of general government expenditure on health as a percentage of total government expenditure (\(\beta_1\)) has no effect on life expectancy (\(H_0: \beta_1 = 0\)). - Alternative Hypothesis (\(H_1\)): There is an effect of the percentage of government expenditure on health on life expectancy (\(H_1: \beta_1 \neq 0\)).

Hypothesis Test Results: - Coefficient Estimate (\(\hat{\beta}_1\)): -0.4027 - Standard Error (SE): 0.0908 - t-value: -4.435 - p-value: \(1.05 \times 10^{-5}\)

Interpretation: - The coefficient estimate \(\hat{\beta}_1 = -0.4027\) represents the estimated change in life expectancy for a one-unit increase in the percentage of government expenditure on health as a percentage of total government expenditure. - The t-value of -4.435 is associated with a p-value of \(1.05 \times 10^{-5}\), which is less than the conventional significance level (e.g., 0.05). - Since the p-value is less than 0.05, we reject the null hypothesis.

Conclusion: There is evidence to suggest that the percentage of government expenditure on health has a statistically significant effect on life expectancy. The negative coefficient indicates that, on average, an increase in this percentage is associated with a decrease in life expectancy.
